dylancode.dev

## What are indices?

+ Indices are *powers*, or *exponents*.
+ Powers are just a way of writing *repeated multiplication*.
+ For example, $a^3$ means $a\times a\times a$.
+ $a$ is the *base*, and $3$ is the *exponent* or *index*.

---

## Laws of indices

There are some rules for working with indices, called the *laws of indices* - they tell us how to simplify expressions with powers.

---

## Multiplying indices with the same base

If we multiply two powers with the same base, we add the indices.

$$
a^x\times a^y=a^{x+y}
$$

---

## Simplify $b^3\times b^4$

+ $a^x\times a^y=a^{x+y}$
+ $a^3\times a^4$
+ $=a^{3+4}$
+ =$a^7$

---

## Simplify $8^5\times 8^{-2}$

+ We are *not* asked to solve this, just to simplify it to find a single power of $8$.
+ $8^5\times 8^{-2}=8^{5+(-2)}$
+ $=8^{5-2}$
+ $=8^3$

---

## Dividing indices with the same base

If we divide two powers with the same base, we subtract the indices.

$$
a^x\div a^y=a^{x-y}
$$

---

## Simplify $c^7\div c^2$

+ $a^x\div a^y=a^{x-y}$
+ $c^7\div c^2$
+ $=c^{7-2}$
+ $=c^5$

---

## Simplify $5^4\div 5^{-1}$

+ Again, we are *not* asked to solve this, just to simplify it to find a single power of $5$.
+ $5^4\div 5^{-1}=5^{4-(-1)}$
+ $=5^{4+1}$
+ $=5^5$

---

## Raising a power to a power

When we raise a power to a power, we multiply the indices.

$$
(a^x)^y=a^{xy}
$$


> We can't simplify $a^{x^y}$, only $(a^x)^y$.

---

## Simplify $(d^3)^4$

+ $(a^x)^y=a^{xy}$
+ $(d^3)^4$
+ $=d^{3\times 4}$
+ $=d^{12}$

---

## Simplify $(2^{-5})^{-3}$

+ $(a^x)^y=a^{xy}$
+ $(2^{-5})^{-3}$
+ $=2^{-5\times -3}$
+ $=2^{15}$

---

## Negative indices

A negative index means the reciprocal of the positive index.

It's like saying "1 over this", the number becomes the denominator of a fraction with 1 as the numerator.

$$
a^{-x}=\frac{1}{a^x}
$$

---

## Simplify $x^{-4}$

+ $x^{-4}=\frac{1}{x^4}$
+ (reciprocol of $x^4$)

---

## Evaluate $10^{-2}$

+ Here, we *do* need to evaluate it (find it as a single number).
+ $10^{-2}=\frac{1}{10^2}$
+ $=\frac{1}{100}$ (because $10^2=10\times 10=100$)

---

## Fractional indices

If we have a number to the power of a fraction $\frac{1}{n}$, this means the $n$th root of the number.

$$
a^{\frac{1}{n}}=\sqrt[n]{a}
$$

A common example is the square root, which is the same as the $1/2$ power:

$$
a^{\frac{1}{2}}=\sqrt{a}
$$

---

## Simplify $16^{\frac{1}{2}}$

+ $16^{\frac{1}{2}}=\sqrt{16}$
+ $=4$ (because $4^2=16$)

---

## Simplify $27^{\frac{1}{3}}$

+ $27^{\frac{1}{3}}=\sqrt[3]{27}$
+ $=3$ (because $3^3=27$)

---

## Fractional indices with a numerator

Sometimes, we'll raise to the power of a fraction which is not just $\frac{1}{n}$, but $\frac{m}{n}$.

The $m$ means we raise it to the power of $m$ after we take the $n$th root.

$$
a^{\frac{m}{n}}={(\sqrt[n]a)^m}
$$

---

## Simplify $8^{\frac{2}{3}}$

+ $8^{\frac{2}{3}}={(\sqrt[3]{8})^2}$
+ $={3^2}$ (because $\sqrt[3]{8}=2$)
+ $=9$

---

## Simplify $27^{\frac43}$

+ $27^{\frac{4}{3}}={(\sqrt[3]{27})^4}$
+ $={3^4}$ (because $\sqrt[3]{27}=3$)
+ $=81$

---

## Standard index results

There's some numbers which, when we use them in powers, give us certain common values:

| Expression | Result        |
| ---------- | ------------- |
| $a^1$      | $a$           |
| $1^a$      | $1$           |
| $a^0$      | $1$           |
| $0^a$      | $0$           |

---

## Standard index results

| Expression | Result        |
| ---------- | ------------- |
| $a^{-1}$   | $\frac{1}{a}$ |
| $(-a)^n$   | $(-1)^n a^n$  |

> $(-a)^n$ is $a^n$ if $n$ is even, and $-a^n$ if $n$ is odd.

---

## Summary

+ Indices are powers, or exponents.
+ Laws of indices tell us how to simplify expressions with powers.
+ Multiplying indices with the same base: add the indices.
+ Dividing indices with the same base: subtract the indices.
+ Raising a power to a power: multiply the indices.
+ Negative indices: take the reciprocal of the positive index.
+ Fractional indices: take the root of the base.
