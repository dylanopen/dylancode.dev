# Hyperbola

A *hyperbola* is actually just an [ellipse](/ellipse) where the $y^2$ term is
negative instead of positive. So the equation of a hyperbola centred at the
origin is:

> $$
> \frac{x^2}{a^2} - \frac{y^2}{b^2} = 1
> $$

Basically, it's where we *subtract* the $y^2$ term instead of *adding* it.

## Asymptotes

Hyperbolas have two asymptotes:
- $y = \frac{b}{a}x$: the positively-sloped asymptote
- $y = -\frac{b}{a}x$: the negatively-sloped asymptote

They basically follow the hyperbola as diagonal lines, and the hyperbola
gets closer and closer to them as you move away from the centre (but never
actually reaches the asymptotes).

> The asymptotes of a hyperbola are at $\frac{b}{a}x$ and $-\frac{b}{a}x$.

## Examples

### Find the equation of the hyperbola with x-intercept 3 and y-intercept 4

- $a = 3$
- $b = 4$
- $\frac{x^2}{3^2} - \frac{y^2}{4^2} = 1$
- $\frac{x^2}{9} - \frac{y^2}{16} = 1$

### Find the equation of the hyperbola with x-intercept 5 and y-intercept 2

- $a = 5$
- $b = 2$
- $\frac{x^2}{5^2} - \frac{y^2}{2^2} = 1$
- $\frac{x^2}{25} - \frac{y^2}{4} = 1$

### A hyperbola has the equation $\frac{x^2}{16} - \frac{y^2}{9} = 1$. Find the x-intercept and y-intercept

- $a^2 = 16 \Rightarrow a = 4$
- $b^2 = 9 \Rightarrow b = 3$
- x-intercept: 4
- y-intercept: 3

### Find the equations of the asymptotes of the hyperbola $\frac{x^2}{16} - \frac{y^2}{9} = 1$

- $a^2 = 16 \Rightarrow a = 4$
- $b^2 = 9 \Rightarrow b = 3$
- $y = \frac{b}{a}x = \frac{3}{4}x$
- $y = -\frac{b}{a}x = -\frac{3}{4}x$

### Find the equations of the asymptotes of the hyperbola $\frac{x^2}{25} - \frac{y^2}{4} = 1$

- $a^2 = 25 \Rightarrow a = 5$
- $b^2 = 4 \Rightarrow b = 2$
- $y = \frac{b}{a}x = \frac{2}{5}x$
- $y = -\frac{b}{a}x = -\frac{2}{5}x$
