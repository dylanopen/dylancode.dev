# Inelastic collision

## What's conserved?

In an inelastic collision:
- [[momentum|Momentum]] is [[conservation of momentum|conserved]]
- [[kinetic energy|Kinetic energy]] is **not** conserved

## What does this mean?

- The sum of the individual objects' momentums before the collision is equal to
  the sum of their momentums after the collision (no [[momentum]] is 'lost')
- The sum of the individual objects' kinetic energies before the collision is
  **greater** than the sum of their kinetic energies after the collision (some
  [[kinetic energy]] is 'lost' to the surroundings, for example as heat or sound)

## What happens to the objects?

- The [[velocity]] of each object is **reduced** after the collision, but they won't
  be swapped.
- The total [[momentum]] of the two objects will be the same before and after the
  collision, but the total [[kinetic energy]] will be less after the collision than
  it was before.

## How does it differ from an [[elastic collision]]?

The key difference is that some energy is *lost* from the system in an inelastic
collision, whereas no energy is lost from the system in an [[elastic collision]].

## Perfectly inelastic collisions

In a perfectly inelastic collision, the two objects stick together after the
collision, and move together at the same [[velocity]].

That's only possible though if the larger object is moving faster than (and
towards) the smaller object before the collision, so that the smaller object
can catch up to the larger object and stick to it.
