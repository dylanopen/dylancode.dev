Maths - Disguised Logarithm EquationsHow do you solve \log_5{125}? \log_5{125} = 3 because 5^3 = 125
This is a disguised logarithm equation where the argument is a power of the base.
Maths - Exponential GraphsWhat shape does an exponential graph y = a^x have? An exponential graph increases rapidly for a>1; it passes through (0,1) and approaches the x-axis as x tends to negative infinity.
Maths - Exponential Graph GradientHow do you find the gradient of an exponential graph at a point? The gradient is given by the derivative dy/dx = a^x \ln(a) for y=a^x.
Maths - Base e ExponentialsWhat is the function y = e^x? y = e^x is an exponential function with base e, where e \approx 2.71828. Its derivative is itself (dy/dx = e^x).
Maths - Converting Exponentials to Base eHow do you convert a^x to base e? a^x = e^{x \ln(a)}.
Maths - Natural Logarithm GraphsWhat is the shape of y = \ln(x)? y = \ln(x) is the inverse of y = e^x; it passes through (1,0), increases slowly, and has a vertical asymptote at x=0.
Maths - Exponential GrowthWhat defines exponential growth? Exponential growth occurs when a quantity increases at a rate proportional to its current value, modeled by y = A e^{kt} with k>0.
Maths - Exponential DecayWhat defines exponential decay? Exponential decay occurs when a quantity decreases at a rate proportional to its current value, modeled by y = A e^{-kt} with k>0.
Maths - Compound InterestHow is compound interest calculated? A = P(1 + r/n)^{nt}, where P is principal, r is annual rate, n is number of compounding periods per year, t is time in years.
Maths - Logarithmic Scale GraphsWhat is a logarithmic scale graph? A graph where one or both axes use a logarithmic scale, so equal distances represent multiplicative changes (e.g., orders of magnitude).
Maths - Binomial Expansion DefinitionWhat is a binomial expansion? The expansion of (a+b)^n into a sum of terms involving powers of a and b with binomial coefficients.
Maths - Binomial CombinationWhat is \binom{n}{r}? \binom{n}{r} is the binomial coefficient, equal to \frac{n!}{r!(n-r)!}, representing the number of combinations of n items taken r at a time.
Maths - Common Binomial ValuesWhat are common binomial values? \binom{n}{0} = 1, \binom{n}{1} = n, \binom{n}{n-1} = n, \binom{n}{n} = 1.

Physics - Gravitational AccelerationWhat is gravitational acceleration? The acceleration of an object due to gravity near Earth’s surface, approximately g = 9.81 \, \text{m/s}^2 downward.
Physics - Free FallWhat is free fall? Motion under the influence of gravity only, with no other forces like air resistance.
Physics - Experiment to Calculate gHow is g measured experimentally? Using methods like timing a falling object or using a pendulum, calculating g from T = 2\pi\sqrt{L/g}.
Physics - Projectile MotionWhat is projectile motion? Motion of an object under gravity with an initial velocity; it follows a parabolic path in two dimensions (horizontal and vertical).
Physics - Vertical Projectile MotionWhat characterizes vertical projectile motion? Only vertical motion under gravity; vertical acceleration = -g, vertical velocity changes linearly.
Physics - Horizontal Projectile MotionWhat is horizontal projectile motion? Initial velocity is horizontal; horizontal velocity is constant, vertical motion is accelerated downward by g.
Physics - Angled Projectile MotionWhat is angled projectile motion? Initial velocity at an angle; horizontal component constant, vertical component has acceleration g.
Physics - FrictionWhat is friction? A force opposing relative motion between surfaces, proportional to normal force; F_f \le \mu N.
Physics - DragWhat is drag? A force resisting motion through a fluid (air or water), depending on speed, area, and fluid density.
Physics - LiftWhat is lift? A force perpendicular to the direction of motion in a fluid, generated by pressure differences (e.g., on wings).
Physics - Terminal VelocityWhat is terminal velocity? The constant speed reached when drag equals gravitational force, resulting in zero net acceleration.
Physics - First Law of MotionState Newton’s First Law of Motion. An object remains at rest or in uniform motion unless acted upon by a net external force.
Physics - Second Law of MotionState Newton’s Second Law of Motion. F = ma; net force equals mass times acceleration.
Physics - Third Law of MotionState Newton’s Third Law of Motion. For every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction; forces occur in pairs.
Physics - MomentumWhat is momentum? p = mv, the product of mass and velocity; a vector quantity.
Physics - Momentum and ForceHow are momentum and force related? F = \Delta p / \Delta t, net force equals rate of change of momentum.
Physics - ImpulseWhat is impulse? J = F \Delta t = \Delta p, change in momentum due to a force over time.
Physics - Conservation of MomentumState conservation of momentum. In a closed system, total momentum before an interaction equals total momentum after.
Physics - Elastic CollisionWhat is an elastic collision? A collision where both momentum and kinetic energy are conserved.
Physics - Inelastic CollisionWhat is an inelastic collision? A collision where momentum is conserved but kinetic energy is not; objects may stick together.
Physics - Explosion MomentumHow does momentum apply in an explosion? Total initial momentum is zero; after explosion, the total momentum of fragments sums to zero.
Physics - DensityWhat is density? \rho = m/V, mass per unit volume.
Physics - Work DoneWhat is work done? W = F \cdot d for constant force in direction of displacement; energy transferred by a force.
Physics - Calculating Work DoneHow is work done calculated? W = Fd \cos \theta, where \theta is the angle between force and displacement.
Physics - Work Done at an AngleHow do you calculate work when force is at an angle? W = Fd \cos \theta, only the component of force in the direction of displacement does work.
Physics - Force-Displacement GraphsWhat does area under a force-displacement graph represent? The area equals the work done by the force.
Physics - PowerWhat is power? P = W/t = Fv, rate of doing work or transferring energy.
Physics - Conservation of EnergyState conservation of energy. Energy cannot be created or destroyed, only transformed from one form to another.
Physics - EfficiencyWhat is efficiency? Efficiency = (useful output energy / total input energy) \times 100\%.
Physics - Energy SourcesWhat are examples of energy sources? Renewable (solar, wind, hydro) and non-renewable (coal, oil, gas, nuclear).
Physics - Energy FormsWhat are forms of energy? Kinetic, potential (gravitational, elastic), thermal, chemical, nuclear, electrical, light, sound.
Physics - Kinetic EnergyWhat is kinetic energy? KE = \frac{1}{2}mv^2, energy due to motion.
Physics - Gravitational Potential EnergyWhat is gravitational potential energy? GPE = mgh, energy due to position in a gravitational field.
Physics - Elastic Potential EnergyWhat is elastic potential energy? EPE = \frac{1}{2}kx^2 for a spring, energy stored due to deformation.
